*Design the game, not the strategy.*

When I was on Wall Street, I once asked my managing director how he created high-performing teams. He described his entire role as that of a game designer. His job was to set up the players, rules, and incentives so that doing great work felt natural. It's stuck with me ever since. I've had the chance to observe a lot of teams (some high-performing, some not) and the better teams tend to have better games.

I believe much of life is about creating good games. Designing a good game requires six decisions:

1. Who's playing?
2. How do you score?
3. Who gets the credit? (the attribution function)
4. What's the payoff?
5. What happens if you break the rules? (the transgression function)
6. What if you do nothing?

Get these right, and participants are far more likely to find the right strategy on their own. Not because the answer is obvious, but because the game makes it worth finding.

## 1. Who's Playing?

Who's on the field? What are the jerseys? If there are too few participants, you often don't have enough people to make meaningful progress. If there are too many, you incentivize Game-of-Thrones-style turf wars where no one can move without stepping on someone else's toes.

## 2. How Do You Score?

Decide what you want to improve and what you don't care about. These need to be explicit. If you don't define what scores points, people will optimize for whatever is most visible, which is rarely what matters most. You want it to be easy to score points doing the right thing and hard to score doing the wrong thing.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">The best scoring systems aren't impossible to game, but they're difficult enough that gaming them requires doing roughly the right thing anyway.</span>

## 3. Who Gets the Credit? (The Attribution Function)

How does someone score in your game? The most common attribution function is "first past the gate" (whoever lands the deal or ships the feature first gets the credit).<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">This is the same principle that drives academic publishing: the first team to publish a result gets the citation.</span>
But bad attribution functions are one of the fastest ways to create fiefdoms: people start credit-hogging, boxing out critical resources, and optimizing for visibility over impact. The best attribution functions reward teams but strongly incentivize the individual. If individuals need to form teams to solve a problem, they naturally will. You want everyone pulling in the same direction.

## 4. What's the Payoff?

There are several strategies for payoff functions that typically work well: percentage payoffs, knock-in barriers, call options, and probabilistic payoffs.

**Percentage payoffs.** Let people keep a cut of the value they create. A salesperson who takes home 20% of every deal they close doesn't need a pep talk, and sales checks are the easiest to write because the revenue is already in the door. The common pathologies here are capping payouts too low and not rewarding people in proportion to the value they generate.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">Capping payouts kills motivation right when it matters most.</span> The problem is scaling: if someone saves the firm a billion dollars, you can't hand them 80% of it.

**Knock-in barriers.** Set aggressive thresholds with outsized payoffs on the other side. If you beat the threshold, the reward is disproportionately large. This creates a clear target that everyone rallies around. Big tech runs on this. If you knock it out of the park, two rapid promotions can increase your compensation by 50-100%. That kind of upside keeps people swinging for the fences.

**Call options.** Cap the downside and let the upside scale. Startup equity is the classic example: you invest your time at a fixed cost, but if the company takes off, your upside is unbounded. This structure works whenever you want people to take big swings without fear of ruin. The worst case is known, but the best case keeps getting better.

**Probabilistic payoffs.** When percentage payoffs can't scale, introduce randomness. Hide bounties across a pool of tasks so people treat every one seriously. If any task could be the one that pays out, quality stays high across the board. Or use a draft system: top performers get first pick of projects next round. Lotteries work because people overweight the possibility of a big win, and even a small amount of randomness keeps participants engaged in ways that predictable rewards can't.

In general, designing convex rewards - where the downside is capped but the upside is very large - incentivizes people to really go for it. But in domains where losses are unbounded, this becomes a serious problem. This is the classic [principal-agent problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal%E2%80%93agent_problem): when the person making the decisions doesn't bear the full cost of those decisions, incentives diverge.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">Finance is the textbook example. If a trader's downside is capped at losing their job but their upside is a percentage of profits, they're incentivized to take enormous risks with other people's money.</span> In those environments, you need participants to share in the downside as well. Without that, you get reckless behavior dressed up as boldness.

## 5. What Happens If You Break the Rules? (The Transgression Function)

The transgression function often shapes the entire game. Define it upfront, and calibrate it carefully. Too punishing and no one wants to play. If the consequence of failure is career death, you won't get many willing participants. Too lenient and the rules mean nothing. Get it right, though, and the transgression function becomes one of the strongest levers you have for shaping how people interact and how hard they push. When payoffs are high, the temptation to game the scoring system is also high. A clear transgression function keeps competition honest and builds camaraderie. People trust the game because they see it being enforced. And if you don't uphold the rules, the game evaporates.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">Nothing kills a high-functioning team faster than the belief that honest work won't be rewarded honestly.</span>

The optimal transgression strategy often looks a lot like [optimal tit-for-tat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Evolution_of_Cooperation):

1. **Be nice** - no first strikes
2. **Be easily provoked** - if someone transgresses, be loud about it
3. **Be forgiving** - don't punish forever
4. **Don't be envious** - focus on your own score, not theirs
5. **Be clear** - mean what you say, say what you mean

## 6. What If You Do Nothing?

The do-nothing option should be uncomfortable. The best games ensure participants have skin in the game.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">Skin in the game means something they lose by standing still.</span>

When inaction is painless, don't be surprised when people choose it.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">A comfortable salary, no visibility, no accountability.</span> But when the do-nothing case has real cost, behavior changes. A salesperson on a 30k base with a 200k upside will hustle because they have no choice. Public leaderboards make inaction visible. Expiring opportunities make delay costly. You don't need to threaten people. You just need to make sure that doing nothing feels worse than trying.

## Recipes

I've seen certain recipes work well for certain environments.

### For Research and Education

The goal is to get people to take very large bets, repeatedly, without fear of risk, so that outsized gains become a matter of time. A friend once told me the best educational environments have "low floors, wide walls, and high ceilings."

- The "floor" should be as low as possible. Failing is expected, and the cost of a dead end should be near zero.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">The "floor" is the do-nothing case (what happens when a participant takes no action).</span>
- The "walls" should be the widest of any environment. Exploration is the whole point, so the transgression function should only kick in for genuinely destructive behavior.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">The "walls" are the transgression function (the boundaries of acceptable play).</span>
- The "ceiling" matters, but it's less about immediate compensation and more about recognition, autonomy, and the opportunity to pursue bigger questions.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">The "ceiling" is the payoff (what excellence gets you).</span>

The known pathology here is freeloading. Pre-2023 big tech ran essentially this recipe, and it predictably produced teams with a few free riders alongside a handful of intensely driven people. But even with that cost, this is the environment that produces the most innovation.

### For Execution and Optimization

Whenever you need people to deliver on a particular goal (this was the default mode in finance) this is probably the way to go. You need a concrete, easy-to-define score function, and often the biggest hurdle is getting it right.

- The "floor" should be deliberately uncomfortable. Careers stall, bonuses disappear, and inaction becomes visible.
- The "walls" are still wide (define the goal, not the path, so people can find creative solutions) but narrower than in research, because the solution needs to converge.
- The "ceiling" should be as high as you can make it. Outsized career acceleration, disproportionate compensation, and the kind of upside that makes people willing to push through the hard parts.
- The players should be loosely defined. No strict hierarchy, no rigid team boundaries. You might have four or five sub-projects all optimizing the same score, and you want people to bounce between them freely.

The whole thing needs to be goal-driven, not methods-driven.<span class="sidenote-number"></span><span class="sidenote">Goal-driven means everyone is optimizing the score and it doesn't matter how they get there. Methods-driven is the opposite: people pick a method and try to optimize different score functions with it. You want work to drive backwards from the score function, not forwards from the method.</span>

And the attribution function should reward the team winning together. You don't want people sniping each other for individual credit. What matters is the team landing results week after week.
